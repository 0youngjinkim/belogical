# grouping-order — Grouping and checking

> Role: form the groups beneath each layer, order them, set their summaries, and check against the three rules.
> Loaded on: document (step 3). Check and diagnostic criteria for the organize and repair routes.
> Distilled from Minto's grouping and summarizing methods. Numeric thresholds are recommended defaults for reducing cognitive load, not absolute rules.

## 1. The three rules — the standard for every check

1. The message above is a substantive summary of the group below.
2. Messages in one group are of the same kind — check that one plural noun (reasons, steps, problems) covers them.
3. Items within a group follow a logical order.

A violation of any rule signals unfinished thinking, not unfinished wording. Don't fix the sentence — go back to the structure.

## 2. Horizontal links are deductive or inductive

A group is deductive or inductive, never a mix of the two. Tell them apart by sentence structure:

- If the second point picks up the subject or predicate of the first, it's **deduction** (major premise → minor premise → therefore).
- If the points don't mention each other and fall under one plural noun, it's **induction** (reasons for, problems with).

Traps to watch for:

- **Disguised deduction**: points laid out in parallel can look inductive while actually forming a causal chain — that's deduction. Misread it as induction and the wrong summary goes on top.
- **A single piece of evidence** cannot carry an induction — reason deductively instead.
- **Keep deductive chains short.** Past three or four steps the reader can't hold them; split the chain, or merge two or three steps into one if comprehension allows. At the Key Line (the layer just below the apex), when you have the choice, induction is the lighter load to deliver.

## 3. The grouping's basis dictates the order

Order is not chosen at will — the analysis that formed the group determines it.

- Grouped by process or cause-and-effect: **time order** — in order of execution.
- A whole divided into parts: **structural order** — in the order the eye sees them.
- Classified by a property: **order of degree** — the strongest in that property first.

If no clear order emerges, the grouping itself is suspect. The common defect in time order is a smuggled-in effect — if one item is the *result* of the others, don't leave it alongside them; lift it a layer up and place the causes beneath it as support. Put yourself in the executor's seat and sketch the steps — missing steps and hidden results will surface.

## 4. MECE and group size

When dividing a whole, the parts must not overlap (mutually exclusive) and must leave nothing out (collectively exhaustive) for the reader to trust the division. When dividing a tangible whole, such as an organization, the classic axes are activity, location, and object — and the parts' order follows the axis you chose.

Four to five items per group is the recommended default. The basis is the limit of short-term memory, not a formal rule — true parallels the reader needn't hold in mind (spec tables, checklists) may exceed it, but when a group of *ideas* passes five, their kinship has usually thinned; bind them one layer up. Grouping is not carving into pieces — it's raising a level.

## 5. Set the summary — no empty assertions

Grouping ends with the summary sentence on top. 'There are N problems' states kind and count but summarizes nothing — when that is the sentence you want to write, stop, find what the items below actually share, and state it substantively ('the problem is an operating structure that cannot delegate authority').

- **Action ideas** (do this) are summarized by the **expected result** the actions jointly produce. Actions group only by their result — try to classify them by kind and they will inevitably overlap. Write the result concretely enough that its achievement can be judged — with figures and dates where possible.
- **Situation ideas** (this is so) are summarized by the meaning of their similarity. Three steps: enumerate; prove they belong together (similar subjects, predicates, or judgments); generalize what the commonality means. If words won't capture it, diagram the relationships as a springboard for the inductive leap.

Setting the summary often surfaces the writer's real conclusion — one they hadn't yet seen. The summary is not decoration for the reader; it is the step that completes the thinking.

## 6. Traps in lists

- Don't group weakly related points just because they look alike.
- After any list, add one substantive summary sentence stating what binds the items.
- Use bullets only for true parallels (specs, procedures, checklists).
- Distinguish mere information from ideas. Information enters the document only when it joins other points in supporting a higher point.

## 7. Only as much precision as the logic warrants

There is no need to run this whole procedure at full precision every time. Readers scrutinize the full structure only when they need to, so a somewhat looser summary is acceptable where you judge the logic sound. Precision is not the goal; the reader's understanding is.
