# scqa-intro — Designing the introduction

> Role: design an introduction that starts where the reader already stands and carries them to the apex without resistance.
> Loaded on: document (step 2)
> Distilled from Minto's SCQA method. Length and format guidance are recommended defaults.

## 1. The introduction is a story

The reader of a standalone document holds no question yet. The introduction creates it: establish a familiar **Situation (S)**, disturb it with a **Complication (C)** so a **Question (Q)** forms in the reader's mind, and let the entire body be the **Answer (A)**. The Answer is the apex of the pyramid; S, C, and Q are the story wrapped around it.

## 2. What the introduction must not do

The introduction is where you remind the reader of what they already know or can be assumed to know.

- Don't pile up new information.
- Don't argue — evidence, figures, and tables wait for the body.
- Don't raise anything contested — common ground comes first.

## 3. How to write the three elements

- **Situation**: start from the subject, and include only undisputed facts the reader will nod along to. If you don't want to open with the subject, that's a signal the subject is wrong.
- **Complication**: shake up the situation with 'and then what happened?'. There are three types — a problem arose or went wrong, something changed, someone disagreed.
- **Question**: exactly one per document. If it looks like two ('Should we enter? How?'), fold them into one ('How should we enter?'). Most documents ultimately answer one of four questions — What should we do / How should we do it / Should we do it / Why did it happen.

## 4. Vary the order for tone

Always conceive the elements starting from the Situation, but the finished pieces can be rearranged.

| Order | Tone |
|---|---|
| Situation → Complication → Answer | Standard — safe |
| Answer → Situation → Complication | Direct — when you want to lead with the conclusion |
| Complication → Situation → Answer | Concerned — leads with what's at stake |
| Question → Situation → Complication | Aggressive — when you want to press the problem hard |

## 5. Length is set by the distance to the reader

Enough for the reader to be confident they stand where the writer stands — no more. Two or three paragraphs usually suffice; for a close reader who shares the context, one sentence can do. An introduction that dwells on what the reader already knows is itself a defect.

## 6. Stock skeletons by document type

As a genre hardens, its Situation–Complication–Question hardens with it. The skeletons below are examples of that pattern — derive skeletons for other genres the same way. Even when the question never appears in the text, the writer writes it down clearly before writing.

| Type | Situation | Complication | (Question) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Directive | We do X | It must be done Y way | What is Y way? |
| Change announcement | We have been using X | It changes as of Y | What do I need to do? |
| README | You have problem X | This tool solves it | What is it and how do I use it? |
| Proposal | You have a problem | You've decided to bring in outside help | Are we the right ones? |
| Progress report | We agreed to do X | You asked me to look into Y | What did you find? |

## 7. Supporting devices

- In long documents, attach a short Situation–Complication–Question to each Key Line point, reminding the reader how it relates to the whole and to the point before it.
- If the question won't come into focus, trace backward from the body: why does the reader need to know this → because it answers which question → and what gave rise to that question?

## 8. What the story form buys

The same facts, told as a story, lend authenticity to the interpretation, so the reader comes to understand the situation in the same frame as the writer. The introduction is more than a hook — it builds the frame in which the reader perceives the situation.
