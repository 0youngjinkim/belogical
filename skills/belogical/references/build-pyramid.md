# build-pyramid — Building the pyramid

> Role: set the one-sentence apex and complete the pyramid layer by layer.
> Loaded on: document (step 1), organize (step 1)
> Distilled from the construction procedure in Minto's *The Pyramid Principle*.

## Principle: structure before prose

Don't go straight to prose. Lay the ideas out as a pyramid and check it first, then write. Once thoughts are committed to type, attachment sets in and structural changes get hard; a structural flaw caught late means a full rewrite.

## 1. Set the apex box

The apex is one sentence that meets all of these:

- A complete sentence with a subject and a predicate — not a title or a noun phrase ('market entry strategy').
- Answers one question the reader has. An answer without a question cannot be an apex.
- Tells the reader something they don't yet know. What they already know is material for the introduction.

The apex may be conditional. If the answer honestly splits on the reader's situation, put the single variable that splits it into the apex sentence and give a decision per branch ('if A, then P; if B, then Q'). This keeps the conclusion-first principle while falling into neither false certainty nor mushy both-sides-ism. But split on one axis only — the one that divides the thinking; trying to enumerate every case blurs the apex.

## 2. Try top-down first

Top-down is easier because you start from what you can be sure of (the subject, the reader). Answer five questions in order to fill the apex and the introduction:

1. What subject am I addressing?
2. What question does the reader have about it?
3. What is the answer to that question?
4. What Situation (what the reader already knows) do I start from?
5. What Complication (tension) gave rise to the question?

If you can't pin down 1–2, drop down to 4–5 first and work backward. Start the Situation from what the reader will accept without dispute, and find the Complication by imagining the reader's 'so what?'. If the question the Complication produces matches the question you assumed in 2, that confirms the point matters to the reader.

## 3. If the apex won't stand, switch to bottom-up

When the subject or the reader's question is uncertain, climb up from the material:

1. Write down every point you want to make.
2. Work out the relationships between them (causal, similarity). Merge items that in effect say the same thing; diagram the causal chains to expose missing points.
3. Derive the conclusion from the relationships.

Once the conclusion emerges, always return to the introduction and connect it to background the reader already knows. Even a conclusion that climbed up from the details must be repackaged as the answer to the reader's question before it will be taken in.

## 4. Expand downward layer by layer

Write down the questions the apex sentence will raise in the reader ('Why? How?'), and make the group one level down answer them. The answer to 'Why?' is always a reason — a point that is not a reason gets cut or fixed. Repeat the same question-and-answer at each subpoint, descending until no logical question remains for the reader.

The goal is comprehension, not agreement. The reader may reject the conclusion — but must never be unsure what is being claimed.

## 5. Check the groups

When each layer is complete, check it against the three rules in grouping-order.md. At the Key Line (the layer just below the apex), if both deduction and induction are open to you, prefer induction — a deductive chain makes the reader hold each step in sequence, while an inductive parallel registers at a glance.
