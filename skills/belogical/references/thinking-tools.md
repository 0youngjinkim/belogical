# thinking-tools — Thinking tools

> Role: before a conclusion stands, expose the relationships in tangled ideas and material to help derive one.
> Loaded on: organize (step 2)
> Distilled from Minto's thinking tools.

## 1. Define the problem — material for the introduction

A problem is the gap between the current result and the desired result. Write four elements concretely:

1. Starting point — the situation while it was still stable
2. Disturbing event — an external factor, an internal factor, or a new realization
3. R1 — the undesired result it produced
4. R2 — the desired result. Write it concretely enough that its achievement can later be judged — with figures and dates where possible. A vague R2 leaves no standard for comparing solutions.

The frame does double duty: it solves the problem and, as written, becomes the introduction — the starting point and the disturbing event form the Situation (S) and Complication (C). Of the sequenced analysis questions, the answers to 'is there a problem / where does it lie' go to the introduction, and the answers to 'why does it exist / what could we do / what should we do' become the body's key points.

Before writing, gauge how far the reader has already come in solving the problem — done nothing yet? knows the cause? already chose a solution? Addressing a stage the reader has already passed is old news.

## 2. Fix relationships as structure

When concepts and points are entangled, don't reach straight for prose. Prose presses relationships into a line, plausibly papering over unresolved structure. Pull out the key nouns and write the relationships between them explicitly — a notation like "A →(causes)→ B", a short outline, a tree. Left as a flat list, entangled points like cost, revenue, and price force the reader to compute the relationships themselves; sorted into a tree, the interrelations are fixed and where the conclusion comes from is immediately visible. A skeleton of relationships is enough — no fine detail needed — and only once the structure holds do you move to language.

It works in reverse too. A bloated sentence is usually one simple relationship between nouns dressed up as something difficult; decompose it into nouns and relations and the original clear message comes back.

Clarity is not a writing technique but the completeness of the thinking that preceded it. If the report isn't clear, the thinking wasn't done early enough.
